# Lie group symmetries

## Two symmetries of the galactic potential

The gravitational potential of a galaxy transitions between two distinct symmetry regimes ([Binney & Tremaine 2008](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691130279/galactic-dynamics)):

**The spherical bulge.** Near the center, the potential is approximately spherical: $\Phi = \Phi(r)$. It is invariant under all three-dimensional rotations. The symmetry group is $SO(3)$, and the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ is generated by the three components of angular momentum $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}$, with Poisson brackets:

$$
\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}\, L_k
$$

The **Casimir invariant** of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ -- the unique scalar that commutes with all three generators ([Hall 2015, S6.4](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-13467-3)) -- is the squared total angular momentum:

$$
\mathcal{C} = L^2 = L_x^2 + L_y^2 + L_z^2
$$

In a spherical potential, $L^2$ is exactly conserved. The orbital plane is fixed.

**The flattened disk.** Further out, the disk dominates and the potential depends on height: $\Phi = \Phi(R, z)$. Only rotations about the symmetry axis leave the potential unchanged. The symmetry group reduces to $SO(2) \subset SO(3)$, generated by $L_z$ alone ([Binney & Tremaine 2008, Ch. 3](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691130279/galactic-dynamics)).

The orbital plane is no longer fixed -- the star oscillates vertically. The components $L_x$ and $L_y$ are no longer conserved, and neither is $L^2$. Only $L_z$ survives.

This is a **symmetry breaking**: $SO(3) \to SO(2)$.

## The Deformed Casimir

In the theory of Lie algebra deformations ([Gilmore 2005, Ch. 13](https://store.doverpublications.com/products/9780486462752)), a Casimir invariant does not simply vanish when the algebra is deformed. Instead, it **transforms** into a new invariant of the deformed algebra -- a _deformed Casimir_ -- that reduces to the original in the undeformed limit.

When disk flattening breaks $\mathfrak{so}(3) \to \mathfrak{so}(2)$, the Casimir $L^2$ is lost. However, the geometric structure of the potential (the Staeckel separability) defines a new invariant $I_3$ that:

1. Reduces to $L^2/2$ in the spherical limit ($\Delta \to 0$)
2. Commutes with the surviving $SO(2)$ generator ($L_z$)
3. Governs the vertical oscillation amplitude in the flattened disk

This $I_3$ -- and its associated action **$J_z$** -- is the deformed Casimir of the galactic symmetry breaking.

## The Role of the Bar

A galactic bar is a **planar, non-axisymmetric perturbation**. It breaks the $SO(2)$ symmetry of the disk, exerting torques that change $L_z$ and drive **radial migration**.

The bar **does not** commute with $L_z$, nor is it "within" the $SO(2)$ subalgebra. Its defining physical effect is to break the symmetry that $L_z$ generates.

### Why is $J_z$ still conserved?

If the bar breaks the symmetry, why does $J_z$ remain unchanged? The answer is twofold:

1. **Adiabatic Invariance.** The bar perturbation typically changes on orbital timescales, while the vertical oscillation frequency $\nu_z$ is much higher ($\nu_z \gg \Omega$). This separation of timescales ensures that $J_z$ is preserved as an adiabatic invariant ([Binney & Tremaine 2008](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691130279/galactic-dynamics)).
2. **Planar Decoupling.** Because the bar is a **planar perturbation**, it acts primarily on the $x$ and $y$ degrees of freedom. In the Staeckel approximation, the vertical motion in the confocal coordinate $\nu$ remains decoupled from the planar torques.

The "algebraic framework" presented here does not claim that the bar _cannot_ change $J_z$ due to symmetry. Rather, it identifies $J_z$ as the **correct coordinate-independent quantity** that should be conserved during migration. As a star moves from a spherical regime to a flattened one, its "vertical" motion must be re-evaluated; the deformed Casimir provides the geometric connection that allows $J_z$ to be compared across different fibers of the galactic bundle.
