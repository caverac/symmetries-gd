# Lie group symmetries

## Two symmetries of the galactic potential

The gravitational potential of a galaxy has two distinct symmetry regimes ([Binney & Tremaine 2008](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691130279/galactic-dynamics)):

**The spherical bulge.** Near the center, the potential is approximately spherical: $\Phi = \Phi(r)$. It is invariant under all three-dimensional rotations. The symmetry group is $SO(3)$, and the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ is generated by the three components of angular momentum $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}$, with Poisson brackets:

$$
\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}\, L_k
$$

The **Casimir invariant** of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ -- the unique scalar that commutes with all three generators ([Hall 2015, S6.4](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-13467-3)) -- is the squared total angular momentum:

$$
\mathcal{C} = L^2 = L_x^2 + L_y^2 + L_z^2
$$

In a spherical potential, $L^2$ is exactly conserved. The orbital plane is fixed.

**The flattened disk.** Further out, the disk dominates and the potential depends on height: $\Phi = \Phi(R, z)$. Only rotations about the symmetry axis leave the potential unchanged. The symmetry group reduces to $SO(2) \subset SO(3)$, generated by $L_z$ alone ([Binney & Tremaine 2008, Ch. 3](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691130279/galactic-dynamics)).

The orbital plane is no longer fixed -- the star oscillates vertically. The components $L_x$ and $L_y$ are no longer conserved, and neither is $L^2$. Only $L_z$ survives.

This is a **symmetry breaking**: $SO(3) \to SO(2)$.

## What happens to the Casimir?

In the theory of Lie algebra deformations and contractions ([Gilmore 2005, Ch. 13](https://store.doverpublications.com/products/9780486462752)), a Casimir invariant does not simply vanish when the algebra is deformed. Instead, it **transforms** into a new invariant of the deformed algebra -- a _deformed Casimir_ -- that reduces to the original in the undeformed limit.

This is the central mathematical fact of the project. When disk flattening breaks $\mathfrak{so}(3) \to \mathfrak{so}(2)$, the Casimir $L^2$ is lost. But a deformed Casimir emerges that:

1. Reduces to $L^2$ in the spherical limit
2. Remains conserved in the flattened disk
3. Is invariant under perturbations that act within $SO(2)$ -- including a rotating bar

## The Staeckel focal distance as deformation parameter

The deformation of $\mathfrak{so}(3) \to \mathfrak{so}(2)$ is not arbitrary -- it is governed by the geometry of the potential. The parameter that controls the deformation is the **Staeckel focal distance** $\Delta(R)$.

A potential is called _Staeckel_ if the Hamilton-Jacobi equation separates in confocal ellipsoidal coordinates ([de Zeeuw 1985](https://doi.org/10.1093/mnras/216.2.273)). In three dimensions, these coordinates are parameterized by a focal distance $\Delta$ that sets the shape of the coordinate surfaces: at $\Delta = 0$ the surfaces are spheres (spherical symmetry); as $\Delta$ increases the surfaces flatten into oblate spheroids (axial symmetry).

For a Miyamoto-Nagai disk with scale length $a$ and scale height $b$, the focal distance can be computed from the separability condition ([Batsleer & Dejonghe 1994](https://doi.org/10.1051/0004-6361:20021779); [Famaey & Dejonghe 2003](https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1365-8711.2003.06833.x)):

$$
\Delta^2(R) = -z_\mathrm{f}^2
$$

where $z_\mathrm{f}$ is the focal point of the best-fitting confocal coordinate system at radius $R$. The function $\Delta(R)$ encodes exactly how much the potential departs from spherical symmetry at each radius.

## The Staeckel third integral

Before identifying the deformed Casimir, we need the **third integral** of a Staeckel potential. In confocal ellipsoidal coordinates $(\lambda, \nu, \phi)$ with focal distance $\Delta$, a Staeckel-separable potential admits three isolating integrals: the energy $E$, the angular momentum $L_z$, and a third integral $I_3$ associated with the separation of the $\nu$ (short-axis) coordinate ([de Zeeuw 1985](https://doi.org/10.1093/mnras/216.2.273)):

$$
I_3 = \frac{1}{2}\,\dot{\nu}^2\,(\nu + \Delta^2) + \frac{L_z^2\,(\nu + \Delta^2)}{2\,\nu} + (\nu + \Delta^2)\,\Phi_\nu(\nu)
$$

where $\Phi_\nu(\nu)$ is the separated potential function in the $\nu$ coordinate. This integral exists because the Hamilton-Jacobi equation separates in the confocal coordinates defined by $\Delta$.

### The spherical limit

When $\Delta \to 0$, the confocal coordinates reduce to spherical coordinates: $\lambda \to r^2$, $\nu \to -r^2 \cos^2\theta$. In this limit, the third integral becomes ([de Zeeuw 1985](https://doi.org/10.1093/mnras/216.2.273); [Binney & Tremaine 2008, S3.5](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691130279/galactic-dynamics)):

$$
I_3 \;\xrightarrow{\Delta \to 0}\; \frac{1}{2}\,L^2 + \text{const}
$$

That is, $I_3$ reduces to the Casimir of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$.

### From $I_3$ to $J_z$

The vertical action is the action integral over one complete $\nu$ oscillation:

$$
J_z = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint p_\nu\, d\nu
$$

Since the $\nu$ motion is governed by $I_3$ (together with $E$ and $L_z$), $J_z$ is a function of these three integrals: $J_z = J_z(I_3, E, L_z)$. Both $E$ and $L_z$ are independently conserved in the axisymmetric potential, so the conservation of $I_3$ is equivalent to the conservation of $J_z$: any change in $J_z$ at fixed $E$ and $L_z$ would require a change in $I_3$.

## Identifying the deformed Casimir

We identify $I_3$ -- and by extension $J_z$ -- as the deformed Casimir of the $\mathfrak{so}(3) \to \mathfrak{so}(2)$ breaking. This identification rests on three properties:

1. **Correct limiting behavior.** $I_3 \to L^2/2$ when $\Delta \to 0$, recovering the Casimir of the unbroken algebra.
2. **Commutation with the surviving subalgebra.** $I_3$ commutes with $L_z$ (the generator of $SO(2)$) by construction -- the separation of variables guarantees $\{I_3, L_z\} = 0$.
3. **Parametric control by the deformation.** The quantity $\Delta(R)$ simultaneously controls the coordinate geometry (how oblate the confocal surfaces are) and the algebra (how far from $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ the system has deformed). This is the hallmark of a deformation parameter in the sense of Lie algebra contractions ([Gilmore 2005, Ch. 13](https://store.doverpublications.com/products/9780486462752)).

These are the defining properties of a deformed Casimir: a conserved quantity of the reduced algebra that reduces to the original Casimir in the undeformed limit. We note that this is a **motivated identification** based on matching these properties, rather than a constructive derivation from the Inoenue-Wigner contraction formalism. The [numerical evidence](../results/staeckel-potential) provides independent support.

### Limiting behavior of $J_z$

The limiting behavior of $J_z$ itself is physically transparent:

- **Spherical limit** ($\Delta \to 0$): the confocal coordinates become spherical, and $J_z$ reduces to a function of $L^2$ and $L_z$ -- it encodes the inclination of the orbital plane, which is fixed when $L^2$ is conserved.
- **Flat disk limit** ($\Delta$ large): $J_z$ becomes the standard vertical action, measuring the amplitude of oscillation above and below the midplane.

$J_z$ smoothly interpolates between these limits, governed by $\Delta(R)$. This is evaluated numerically via the Staeckel approximation ([Binney 2012](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-2966.2012.21757.x)), which uses the local focal distance $\Delta(R)$ to define the coordinate system in which the vertical motion separates.

## Why the bar cannot change $J_z$

This is the core of the argument.

A Casimir invariant commutes with _all_ generators of its algebra. In the deformed algebra, $J_z$ commutes with all operations in $SO(2)$ -- that is, with any perturbation that acts within the disk plane.

A galactic bar is precisely such a perturbation. The Dehnen bar potential ([Dehnen 2000](https://doi.org/10.1086/301226)) is a planar, bisymmetric perturbation: it generates torques that change $L_z$ (angular momentum exchange at corotation) and reshuffle radial orbits (changing $J_R$). But these are all operations _within_ the plane -- they are generated by elements of $SO(2)$.

If $J_z$ is the deformed Casimir, it commutes with these generators. The bar can freely change $J_R$ and $L_z$, but **it cannot change $J_z$**.

Under this identification, the protection is not an approximate statement about weak perturbations or adiabatic invariance. It is an algebraic identity: the Casimir commutes with the generators. The same mechanism that protects $L^2$ in a spherical potential would protect $J_z$ in a flattened disk -- both as Casimir invariants of their respective symmetry algebras.

## Relation to the existing literature

The conservation of $J_z$ during bar-driven migration has been demonstrated numerically by multiple groups:

- [Solway, Sellwood & Schoenrich (2012)](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-2966.2012.21608.x) showed that churning at corotation preserves vertical action in N-body disks.
- [Minchev et al. (2012)](https://doi.org/10.1051/0004-6361/201220189) distinguished churning (conserves $J_z$) from blurring (heats vertically), and showed that the disk's vertical structure is shaped by the migration history.
- [Vera-Ciro, D'Onghia & Navarro (2016)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1605.03575) demonstrated that migrated stars retain the vertical oscillation amplitude of their birth radius -- a "provenance bias" that encodes birth environment.
- [Mikkola, McMillan & Hobbs (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1093/mnras/staa364) ran a suite of N-body simulations varying disc-to-halo ratio, confirming that $J_z$ is conserved on average during churning but showing increased scatter for individual particles in disc-dominant, bar-forming systems.
- [Frankel et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.3847/1538-4357/ab910c) fit a parametric model to APOGEE red clump stars, finding that the Milky Way disk undergoes substantial angular momentum diffusion with an order of magnitude less radial heating -- consistent with approximate $J_z$ conservation during churning.

These works established the _fact_ of $J_z$ conservation. The contribution of this project is the _explanation_: $J_z$ is conserved because it is the deformed Casimir of the $\mathfrak{so}(3) \to \mathfrak{so}(2)$ symmetry breaking, and the bar perturbation lies within the unbroken $SO(2)$ subalgebra.

The existing theoretical explanation for $J_z$ conservation is **adiabatic invariance**: the bar perturbation changes on timescales much longer than the vertical oscillation period, so $J_z$ is approximately preserved as a slow parameter ([Binney & Tremaine 2008](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691130279/galactic-dynamics)). However, [Vera-Ciro & D'Onghia (2016)](https://doi.org/10.3847/0004-637X/824/1/39) showed that this approximation degrades after several orbital periods in galaxies with vigorous spiral activity and bar formation. The algebraic argument offered here is fundamentally different: it does not rely on timescale separation but on the commutation of the Casimir with the perturbation generators. This makes the protection exact for any planar perturbation, regardless of its strength or timescale.
